# Futures vs. Swaps: Key Differences

Futures and swaps are both derivative instruments used for hedging and speculation, but they differ significantly in structure, trading venue, and risk profiles.

## Summary Definition
* **Futures:** Standardized, exchange-traded contracts obligating two parties to buy or sell an asset at a predetermined price on a specific date.
* **Swaps:** Customized, over-the-counter (OTC) agreements where two parties privately exchange cash flows or liabilities based on an underlying asset over a set period.

## Core Comparisons

### 1. Trading Location
* **Futures:** Traded on regulated, centralized exchanges (e.g., CME, NSE).
* **Swaps:** Traded Over-the-Counter (OTC) and negotiated privately between institutions, dealers, or corporations.

### 2. Contract Standardization
* **Futures:** Highly standardized in size, expiration date, and asset quality.
* **Swaps:** Fully customizable, allowing parties to tailor payment streams, terms, and tenors to match specific business needs.

### 3. Credit & Default Risk
* **Futures:** Backed by a central clearinghouse that acts as an intermediary, virtually eliminating counterparty default risk.
* **Swaps:** Carry bilateral counterparty risk, meaning if one party defaults, the other may not receive their payments.

### 4. Settlement & Margins
* **Futures:** Require daily maintenance margins (collateral deposits) and are "marked-to-market" daily.
* **Swaps:** Typically do not require initial margin deposits, though counterparties may require collateral depending on credit ratings and exposure size.

### 5. Duration & Payouts
* **Futures:** Usually short-term agreements resulting in a single settlement (cash or physical delivery) at the expiration date.
* **Swaps:** Typically longer-term agreements involving a *series* of future cash flow exchanges (e.g., swapping a fixed interest rate for a floating rate over 5 years).
